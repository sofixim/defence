Abstract
Rhythmic abilities are deeply rooted in the human brain. They originated from our need for social interaction and bonding and have evolved hand-to-hand with communication and linguistic abilities across ages. In the ontogenetic evolution, first experiences with rhythm occur already in the womb, with different rhythmic sources being available to the human fetus. Among sensory modalities, vestibular, tactile, and somatosensory perception (Provasi et al., 2014, VTS) seems to play a crucial role in early rhythm processing, with neural coupling dynamics allowing for VTS and sensorimotor influences on auditory encoding (Trainor et al., 2009; Phillips-Silver and Trainor, 2005; Tichko et al., 2021). However, a restricted corpus of studies specifically focused on VTS and sensorimotor abilities in rhythm and language development. Therefore, the present work aimed at investigating the role of the body through VTS and sensorimotor rhythmic abilities in language development. Togethere, the Theoretical and Experimental (five studies) sections will bring evidence on the role of VTS experiences and sensorimotor abilities across language development.

Study 3: a pilot VTS rhythmic priming for processing language

This study is a pilot to directly test the benefit on processing language when primed by a matching, short-term VTS rhythmic exposure.

Could the Infant vibrotactile system serve as a possible application for transferring the documented effects of rhythm on language processing through short-term exposure to VTS rhythmic primes?

Rhythmic priming tasks have been largely applied to transfer benefits from short-term exposures to musical rhythms across domains (Cason and Schön, 2012; Cason et al., 2015a; Fiveash et al., 2020; Gould et al., 2017; Chern et al., 2018). Moreover, the rhythmic priming effect was found to occur also across different sensory modalities, including auditory to visual (Fotidzis et al., 2018) and visual to auditory signals (Liu et al., 2012; Gould et al., 2018).

However, less is known about the cross-modal influence of haptic inputs (Schneider et al., 2011), especially considering sensorimotor and vestibular modalities which have been investigated only as a cue to spatial orientation before (Lindeman et al., 2003).

Therefore, the main research question of this Study is whether the cross-domain (i.e., musical to language) and cross-sensory (i.e., tactile to auditory) associations between rhythm and language do sustain the possibility to rely on one signal and modality to process the other.

Ten infants (6 females, M age = 632 days, SD = 306,68) took part to this pilot. They were tested with VTS rhythmic primes and auditory linguistic sequences presented in contingency with a visual attentional getter.

Each trial consisted of a priming and a test phase. The rhythmic priming was adminis- tered under the VTS modality through the Infant vibrotactile system placed on the infant seat; then, the linguistic stimulus was auditory presented. Contingent visual stimuli (i.e., colourful checkerboard and a cartoon attentional getter) were presented to attract the infant’s attention allowing the eye-tracker to collect pupil data.

Binary (i.e., 2/4) or ternary (i.e., 3/4) priming were followed by matching or mismatching syllabic sequences, while the colourful checkerboard shown as visual stimulus.

Descriptive statistics (Figure 4.18) show a slight increase in pupil diameter during the test phase for mismatching over matching trials. Specifically, the data distribution, shown by violin (Figure 4.18, left) and density (Figure 4.18, upright plots), is shifted over positive values for pupil diameter in mismatching trials compared to data from the matching condition.

To better explore variations in pupil diame- ter over time, baseline corrected pupillary data were plotted in the time di- mension applying a locally estimated scatter-plot smoothing (Cleveland and Devlin, 1988, loess) that is a non-parametric regression method combining multiple regression models in a k-nearest neighbour, based meta-model (Ja- coby, 2000). The loess method was specified in the geom_smooth function of the ggplot2 package (Wickham, 2016) in R software (R Core Team, 2022).

Given that individuals with dyslexia struggle in synchronizing to an external beat, what are the mechanisms through which a regular rhythmic cue enhances their performances in linguistic tasks?

In this Study, participants were asked to perform a lexical decision task for word and non-word in three different conditions: i) under a baseline setting, ii) under articulatory suppression, and iii) under articulatory suppression with a rhythmic cue .

The sensorimotor entrainment to a regular rhythmic cue should facilitate performances, containing the interfering effects of the articulatory suppression thus aiding the allocation of attentional resources in time.

Working memory, reading difficulties in every-day life, and music education are expected to mediate the effect of sensorimotor entrainment to rhythm on reading.

Participants and Design

Sixty, healthy young adults (37 females, M = 21 age, SD = 2).

Verbal stimuli for the lexical decision task consisted of 48 items from the Italian Battery for the assessment of dyslexia and developmental dysorthography by Sartori et al. (1995).

Rhythmic stimulation was provided via a metronome paced at 2 Hz (120 bpm). To balance the presence of auditory stimulation across conditions, white noise was played in the background.

Self-report questionnaire consisting of 20 items with yes/no answers (Vinegard, 1994, A revised Dyslexia Checklist). Two items asking whether participants received, and for how many years, individual lessons with a musical instrument were added to the checklist.

Short-term exposure to a regular rhythmic cue is found to reduce the cognitive load (but is not sufficient to boost performances) in poor readers performing demanding linguistic tasks.

**Given the substantial involvement of sensorimotor coupling in language processing, could the special case of written language particularly benefit from the effect of sensorimotor entrainment to rhythm?

In this Study, handwriting quality and kinematic will be investigated in the sample of sixty young adults participating in Study 4, called to perform a second experimental section consisting of a dictation task.

This task - adapted from Colombo et al. (2009) - consisted of a list of words auditory presented to and written by participants. Performances will be evaluated in terms of product quality (i.e., legibility), kinematic indexes (i.e., average speed and pressure of stroke), and adherence to the isochrony principle (i.e., the ratio between speed and length of movements).

As in Study 4, the dictation task was performed in a baseline condition, under articulatory suppression, and in presence of a rhythmic cue.

Participants and Design

Participants from Study 4 [i.e., Sixty, healthy young adults (37 females, M = 21 age, SD = 2).] participated in this Study as a second experimental section to the whole project.

Words for the dictation task consisted of 48 items from Colombo et al. (2009), divided into three lists of 16 items each, balanced for syllable number and frequency. The digit span test, self-report difficulties in everyday literacy abilities, and music education were investigated during the first experimental section.

Kinematic measures were collected by adapting the dictation task (Colombo et al., 2009, i.e., paper-and-pencil) to a digitalized version, by means of an iPad Air and an Apple Pencil. The Pencil is sensitive to tilt and pressure and, while using it, a custum-made algorithm collected data on speed, pressure, and length of strokes.

  1. product quality was traditionally assessed in terms of legibility, through a Likert scale, by three independent evaluators blinded to hypothesis and design (Barnett et al., 2018);

  2. kinematic indexes included average speed and average pressure of stroke (Rosenblum et al., 2003; Kushki et al., 2011);

  3. adherence to isochrony (i.e., rhythmic patterning of handwriting) was computed as the ratio between average speed and stroke length (Pagliarini et al., 2015, 2017).

A lower adherence to the isochrony principle of handwriting was significantly predicted by high levels of literacy difficulties (b=-0.00005, SE=0.00001, t=-4.093, p<0.001) and low levels of working memory (b=0.00007, SE=0.00001, t=6.145, p<0.001). By contrast, music education predicted a general increase in velocity (b=0.00594, SE=0.00297, t=1.998, p=0.046). Overall, working memory resulted as the best predictor of adherence.

Hell, yes!

  1. Crucially, rhythm was found to significantly improve writing performances.

  2. Furthermore, music education significantly predicted an overall improvement in writing performance. Therefore, the present Study further confirms the core involvement of rhythm in literacy abilities, bringing new evidence on the effect of short- and long-term exposure to rhythm.

  1. Therefore, results from Study 5 suggest a positive effect of short- and long-term exposure to rhythm on the cognitive mechanisms underlying writing abilities including phonological processing, working memory, and motor control.

## Questions and hypothesis 

<p class="text-success">**Could the Infant vibrotactile system serve as a possible application for transferring the documented effects of rhythm on language processing through short-term exposure to VTS rhythmic primes?**</p>

::: {.grid}


::: {.g-col-3}

```{r}
#| eval: true
#| out-width: 90%
#| fig-align: center

```

::: {.g-col-9}

Rhythmic priming tasks have been largely applied to transfer benefits from short-term exposures to musical rhythms across domains (Cason and Schön, 2012; Cason et al., 2015a; Fiveash et al., 2020; Gould et al., 2017; Chern et al., 2018). Moreover, the rhythmic priming effect was found to occur also across different sensory modalities, including auditory to visual (Fotidzis et al., 2018) and visual to auditory signals (Liu et al., 2012; Gould et al., 2018). 

However, less is known about the cross-modal influence of haptic inputs (Schneider et al., 2011), especially considering sensorimotor and vestibular modalities which have been investigated only as a cue to spatial orientation before (Lindeman et al., 2003).

:::


:::

Therefore, the main research question of this Study is whether the cross-domain (i.e., musical to language) and cross-sensory (i.e., tactile to auditory) associations between rhythm and language do sustain the possibility to rely on one signal and modality to process the other. 


## Method 

Ten infants (6 females, M age = 632 days, SD = 306,68) took part to this pilot. They were tested with VTS rhythmic primes and auditory linguistic sequences presented in contingency with a visual attentional getter.

# Study 4: influence of sensorimotor rhythm on reading abilities

In this Study, the effect of a sensorimotor entrainment to rhythm on reading abilities is evaluated in sixty, healthy young adults.

## Questions and hypothesis 

<p class="text-success">**Given that individuals with dyslexia struggle in synchronizing to an external beat, what are the mechanisms through which a regular rhythmic cue enhances their performances in linguistic tasks?**</p>

::: {.grid}


::: {.g-col-3}

In this Study, participants were asked to perform a **lexical decision task** for word and non-word in three different conditions: i) under a baseline setting, ii) under **articulatory suppression**, and iii) under articulatory suppression with a **rhythmic cue** . 

The sensorimotor entrainment to a regular rhythmic cue should facilitate performances, containing the interfering effects of the articulatory suppression thus aiding the allocation of attentional resources in time. 

**Working memory**, **reading difficulties** in every-day life, and **music education** are expected to mediate the effect of sensorimotor entrainment to rhythm on reading.

In this Study, **handwriting quality and kinematic** will be investigated in the sample of sixty young adults participating in Study 4, called to perform a second experimental section consisting of a **dictation task**.

Performances will be evaluated in terms of **product quality** (i.e., legibility), **kinematic indexes** (i.e., average speed and pressure of stroke), and adherence to the **isochrony principle** (i.e., the ratio between speed and length of movements).

## Method

<h5>Participants and Design</h5>

The **digit span test**, self-report difficulties in everyday **literacy abilities**, and **music education** were investigated during the first experimental section.
